About

We speak, gesture, write, touch to communicate; we create signals to represent reality, exploiting different semiotic resources and modalities. Yet each of these resources is inherited: a code already in place between sender and receiver, its signals and their meanings fixed before any exchange begins. This leaves a prior question unasked: how do we communicate what no sign yet stands for, coordinate on a meaning no one can yet name? Communication is the problem of making the unknown mutually known, and every established system is what remains once that problem has been solved.

Experimental semiotics returns to the point before any code exists, placing people in the position of having to build a system with no code to fall back on. Through this approach, scientists study the emergence and evolution of human communication systems under controlled laboratory conditions. These studies track how cognitive and bodily resources are recruited as systems are negotiated in interaction and reshaped across transmission.

The field sits at the intersection of cognitive science, linguistics, and cultural evolution. It bears on questions of language origin and evolution, and more specifically, how iconic signals become conventional symbols, under what conditions combinatorial structure emerges, and how modality and articulator shape the resulting systems.

This index gathers experimental studies in the field, organized by physical articulator, perceptual modality, transmission paradigm, and topic.
